## Bursting Bubbles

By now most people have formed an opinion about the future of AI, and it tends to fall into one of two camps. In one are those calling AI a bubble, set to burst any minute, with the other insisting that there's no bubble and that the growth is only just getting started.

But both perspectives are wrong, because they are framed as a binary. The truth is a little more nuanced, but not unprecedented.

Where AI goes next is neither crash nor continual growth, and fortunately (or unfortunately, which is an equally valid perspective) we have enough facts and historical precedent to tell us exactly what will happen instead.

## End of the Free Ride

Predictions about AI as bubble or infinite money printer aren't new, but the past eight months have been a slow turning point, with a hard pivot at the start of this month.

On the first of June 2026, [GitHub Copilot moved to usage-based billing](https://github.blog/news-insights/company-news/github-copilot-is-moving-to-usage-based-billing/), which carried an expected increase in price. Less expected was just how much of an increase, [as reported by TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/30/what-a-joke-github-copilots-new-token-based-billing-spurs-consternation-among-devs/), users saw jumps from $29 a month to $750, and in the time since we've seen bills in the thousands, for _individuals_. That's the difference between a streaming service and a mortgage.

This isn't your typical price increase, and it isn't a frog-in-boiling water scenario ([GitHub telegraphed it](https://github.com/github/copilot-billing-preview)), and unsurprisingly has led to outrage - [the Community Discussion TechCrunch reported on got more than 900 downvotes](https://github.com/orgs/community/discussions/192948). Two weeks in and people are still understandably angry, but perhaps most interesting are the number of people who, while disappointed and even angry, _understand_. Amidst the rage cancellations and migrations to other tools are plenty of people quietly saying this is fair, we just can't afford it.

## We've seen this before (but not where you think)

It's easy to reach for crypto as a comparison, and to a lesser extent IoT, but neither are the best historical parallel.

IoT was, and is, a hobby. The name gives it away: 'internet of things' is not a phrase serious people use to describe serious infrastructure. It's the domain of makers and tinkerers, and that's exactly what it was meant to be. Granted, there are real enterprise IoT use cases, including some I've had exposure to. Such as in healthcare, where I spent a decade, which is a prime and frankly terrifying example, and more recently within a buildings intelligence company. But attempts to turn it into the next big thing went nowhere; the uses are real, but niche, both commercially and as a hobby. It was never a transformational technology that everyone could capitalise on.

Crypto was, in my opinion, different in that the technology genuinely was interesting, only we never got to find out just _how_ interesting, because big money pumped and dumped it into a joke before the actual use cases (at least for Blockchain more broadly) saw the light of day.  Instead we got a wealth-transfer machine, and yes some people made a lot of money, but unless you're a government, literally printing money, when one person makes a fortune, someone else loses one. Crypto wiped out ordinary people's savings while those gambling with the world's money got rich no matter which way it fell. *(That's a whole other discussion, but I won't rehash Taleb's* [Skin in the Game](https://www.penguin.com.au/books/skin-in-the-game-9780141982656) *here.)*

Both have several parallels with AI, but neither is the best model for what comes next for one important reason: _ubiquity_.

Crypto was huge, but not ubiquitous. IoT _wasn't_ huge, and perhaps because it never blew up out of its niche it gets taken more seriously now. But AI is different in that it's something anyone and everyone can use, and does. Most people are sick to death of having it shoved down their throat, but it is broadly *useful* in a way crypto and IoT never were for ordinary folks. It helps with work, with creativity, and with the small administrative friction of being alive.

And that's exactly why the bubble-burst crowd is wrong. Crypto and IoT could deflate without anyone much noticing because, for the ordinary person, there was nothing real underneath to miss. When the speculative money left crypto, the value left with it, because the value *was* the speculation. With AI, though, the utility is real and it's already in tens of millions of hands. You don't get a crypto-style pop for something that actually works and everyone is already using. But the no-bubble crowd is equally wrong, because the money sloshing around it is not rational or sustainable either. The froth is real, it's just that when it blows off, the substance underneath remains.

But there _is_ a historical parallel, one where the technology was genuinely useful, and genuinely ubiquitous. And it helps show us exactly what happens next.

## The smartphone arc

Before the iPhone, smartphones were an executive toy. The Nokia Communicator, the various PalmPilot derivatives - these were the form factors, and they belonged to the wealthy and the senior. And I'm sure some people found ways to eke productivity out of them, but they were largely just very expensive toys.

BlackBerry changed things though. The BlackBerry was an *actual* phone, a sensible one you could actually use as a phone. But through BlackBerry Enterprise Server and that beloved keyboard, it was also a real email tool. It was aspirational in a way the earlier devices weren't. Not everyone wanted one, but it *was* a status symbol.

Then the iPhone made the smartphone something *everyone* wanted, and that changed personal computing entirely. I'm fond of [Steve Ballmer's 2007 interview](https://www.thurrott.com/mobile/ios/84810/steve-ballmer-right-iphone-premium) where he criticised the iPhone for lacking a keyboard and being a poor email device. People like to mock that now, but he was absolutely right: touchscreen keyboards _are_ terrible, but he never said that would doom the iPhone, he said we would have to wait and see whether it mattered. And, of course, it didn't.

They came with mobile plans, sometimes subsidised, sometimes not. Changes to the mobile data pricing model played a big part, but those changes were driven by pressure from Apple and are inseparable. And soon enough, millions of people could justify having an internet connected computer in their pocket, and smartphones became ubiquitous.

Then came the iPad, and the clones that followed, but they were different. People bought tablets with their own money, believing they'd get the same value or more as they did from their smartphones. They brought them into work and demanded their employers build value around a personal purchase. But the market wasn't ready. The software ecosystem didn't exist.

But [when things get big, enterprise wants in](https://www.beerdriven.dev/episodes/59/). 'Enterprise mobility' became an industry overnight. The corporate box-shifters and systems integrators pushed MDM and enterprise Wi-Fi like it was the future. But none of it mattered, because the software wasn't there yet. The only people who got rich in that gold rush were the ones selling shovels.

As always, you can (and should) follow the money. Sales of smartphones aren't going anywhere, but tablets are barely a blip now. But for social proof, think about meetings. In the first few years since the iPad launched, how many people did you see bring a tablet into a meeting? And how about now, 16 years on? It's not zero today, but the people who do either use them with a keyboard or a stylus - the very thing Steve Jobs supposedly forbade. Though he was misunderstood: "if you see a stylus, they blew it" was about *navigation*, not text input. Don't forget the Newton, which he killed due to mismanagement, _not_ because he didn't like the stylus.

Neither smartphones or tablets became the productivity revolution anyone sold. They became personal devices that occasionally do work, on the user's terms, with the user's accessories.

That's the smartphone arc: a technology enterprise tried to capitalise on, that found its real value *with individuals*.

## Why the corporate AI vision didn't deliver

I've written about this in more depth [on the Arinco blog](https://arinco.com.au/blog/why-youre-not-seeing-ai-business-value/), so I'll keep it brief here. The core finding from [MIT's NANDA initiative](https://aimagazine.com/news/mit-why-95-of-enterprise-ai-investments-fail-to-deliver), their "GenAI Divide" report, is that around 95% of enterprise AI pilots fail to deliver measurable impact on the bottom line.

That's the part people seem to focus on, but the more interesting point is that the same research finds that at an individual level, for people using ChatGPT and Copilot to get their actual jobs done, AI is working. They called it the "shadow AI economy." The technology works; it just works for people, not for org charts.

To say that corporate AI has failed isn't fair. Plenty of organisations are seeing genuine gains. What's actually happened, [as we discussed on the podcast](https://www.beerdriven.dev/episodes/62/), is that initiatives that saw AI as a tool that empowers individuals worked. Those that saw it as an opportunity for cost reduction (and in corporate-speak, 'cost saving' is a euphemism for headcount) did not.

## The economics don't work, and now everyone knows it

It's not just Copilot.

OpenAI has [committed to something on the order of $1.4 trillion](https://www.rdworldonline.com/facing-14b-losses-in-2026-openai-is-now-seeking-100b-in-funding-but-can-it-ever-turn-a-profit/) in compute and energy deals against a tiny fraction of that in revenue. The renegotiated Microsoft arrangement reportedly caps the revenue-share trajectory dramatically lower than it once was. Burn projections run into the tens of billions a year, with cash-flow positivity not pencilled in until the end of the decade. And when [investors did the maths](https://fortune.com/2026/01/29/microsoft-stock-openai-sam-altman-debt-capital-expenditure-ai-oracle/) on how much of Microsoft's own remaining performance obligations were tethered to OpenAI, a staggering amount of market value evaporated in a single stretch.

It's tempting to say OpenAI is in a death spiral. It isn't,it has enormous usage and fast-growing revenue, but it *is* cornered: structurally exposed in a way its own partners are publicly backing away from. And cornered is more interesting than dead, because cornered animals are unpredictable.

## The warehouses full of chips nobody is using

There's another giant pile of unrealised revenue. The [hyperscalers have committed hundreds of billions](https://www.ciodive.com/news/hyperscalers-two-thirds-data-center-capacity-2031/817016/) for 2026 alone, but only a sliver of the planned data-centre [capacity](https://tech-insider.org/us-ai-data-center-delays-cancellations-7gw-capacity-crisis-2026/) is actually under active construction. The bottleneck isn't hardware, it's _energy_: transformers, switchgear, and grid connections that take five to seven years to build. (Yes, the terrifying amount of energy AI is already guzzling is a drop in the ocean compared to what's planned).

So the GPUs and the high-bandwidth memory are [stockpiled, sitting in warehouses, driving up prices for everyone else, waiting for electricity infrastructure that doesn't exist](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/microsoft-ceo-says-the-company-doesnt-have-enough-electricity-to-install-all-the-ai-gpus-in-its-inventory-you-may-actually-have-a-bunch-of-chips-sitting-in-inventory-that-i-cant-plug-in). The gap is staggering: vast tracts of announced capacity, only a fraction of it actually drawing power. We are pricing consumers out of memory and graphics hardware to build data centres that can't be switched on.

## The hardware tell: they just pivoted to your desk

I said earlier you should follow the money, so let's do that. At GTC, [NVIDIA and Microsoft announced the RTX Spark](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-microsoft-windows-pcs-agents-rtx-spark), built on the DGX Spark / GB10 silicon, and [Microsoft's own blog](https://blogs.windows.com/windowsexperience/2026/05/31/introducing-a-powerful-new-chapter-for-windows-pcs-accelerated-by-nvidia-rtx-spark/) framed it in two words: *personal AI*. [Jensen Huang said NVIDIA's goal is to "reinvent the PC"](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/jensen-huang-says-nvidia-wants-to-reinvent-the-single-most-important-tool-of-humanity-with-rtx-spark-nvidia-ceo-touts-support-of-literally-every-computer-maker-in-the-world-for-its-agentic-ai-pc-platform), with focus "100% on Windows."

Microsoft and NVIDIA. One dominates enterprise after two decades of failing to matter in consumer; the other dominates consumer silicon while desperate to be taken seriously in enterprise.

The two companies whose subsidised infrastructure made OpenAI viable are now both betting publicly that the long term value of AI lives on the user's own device. Built into the computers we're going to buy anyway over the next decade, rather than rented from someone else's cloud forever.

That leaves OpenAI without the two partners it most depended on. Microsoft and NVIDIA didn't say a word against them, they just announced where the next decade of investment is going, and it isn't into renting frontier models to consumers. It's into putting the model on your desk.

## Consumers pay the AI bill (just not in cash)

The enterprise subsidy isn't coming, and the real cost has arrived, but someone still has to pay. And we already know how this goes, because Google taught us, and social media confirmed it: the consumer doesn't pay, they *become the product.*

A surprising number of people have proven willing to pay for AI. More than I'd have guessed, and across a broader demographic than just tech enthusiasts. Granted, I'm overexposed to people predisposed and able to pay, and there's a survivorship bias in that the people talking about AI are by definition the ones who use it. But willingness among the few isn't the same as the mass adoption AI needs to be commercially sustainable. And mass-market consumers have been trained for two decades by Google and the social platforms to expect that what they consume online is free. So we'll get the model those two decades built: not the one that serves the user best, but the one that monetises them most reliably.

It's already started. The [sycophancy in GPT-4o](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cn4jnwdvg9qo), earlier last year, was driven by feedback and obviously was not well received, but by the end of the year I started to feel that ChatGPT was noticeably different. Not a change in the model, but a change in the algorithm: reflexive follow-up questions to keep me typing, the sense of being held on the line rather than helped and let go. It felt a lot like something we've all grown fed up of.

It's anecdotal, but there's a compelling story behind it. In September 2025, [OpenAI had paid $1.1 billion for Statsig](https://www.forbes.com/sites/justinwarren/2022/04/20/statsig-experimentation-product-raises-43-million-series-b/), a platform built around A/B testing and engagement optimisation of which they were already a customer. They [installed its founder](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/09/02/openai-buys-statsig-for-1point1-billion-hires-ceo-as-applications-exec.html), who spent a decade [working on consumer engagement engineering at Facebook](https://www.flagsmith.com/blog/why-openai-acquired-statsig), as CTO of Applications, in charge of ChatGPT itself.

On its own, this at least raises an eyebrow. But it wasn't alone. It was followed by [an announcement](https://openai.com/index/our-approach-to-advertising-and-expanding-access/) in January that ChatGPT would start including ads in the free and a newly announced $8 a month tier. OpenAI promised the ads would sit in the UI, *adjacent* to the model's responses, clearly separated from them, and relevant. [That's already a broken promise](https://www.pcmag.com/news/im-seeing-ads-in-chatgpt-openai-broke-its-promise-what-they-look-like), but even if it wasn't, let's not forget that's exactly how Google started. The ads, which were always in your face but used to be easily distinguishable from search results, now fill the first page.

Google won by having the best index; you could find anything, if you refined your google-fu. Then SEO arrived, and the burden inverted. It was no longer Google's job to find everything; it was *your* job to make your content findable. We should have read that as the warning it was. We didn't. And now we have full enshittification: you don't optimise to rank, you *pay* to rank.

The terms 'AEO' and 'AIO' — answer-engine and AI optimisation — are already being sold. Remember the shovel-merchants from the smartphone gold rush, the ones who got rich selling MDM and enterprise Wi-Fi into a market that wasn't ready? They're back, and this time they're promising to make sure your brand surfaces when a customer asks an AI for options. And Cambridge Analytica already showed us who's willing to exploit a pipe like that. So no, I don't think we can honestly claim LLM outputs *haven't already* been shaped by commercial and political interests. The ads in the margin are the polite phase. Google took two decades to go fully pay-to-play. I don't think AI has that long a tail.

## Meanwhile, the consumer revolt is already underway

The increasingly hopeful consumer response is being driven from exactly the niche NVIDIA appears to be distancing itself from: gaming. While Jensen Huang has not explicitly stated that he wants out of gaming (contrary to popular opinion; in fact he has said the opposite), NVIDIA has unquestionably and decisively deprioritised the consumer gaming GPU in favour of high-margin AI silicon, and the RTX Spark makes the personal-AI bet explicit.

Independent of GPU prices, gamers are fed up. The big studios have consolidated, products are driven by profit rather than craft, and consumers are turning to indie and AA studios, to European and Japanese houses. They don't want live-service games. They didn't appreciate being told [by Ubisoft](https://www.ign.com/articles/ubisoft-exec-says-gamers-need-to-get-comfortable-not-owning-their-games-for-subscriptions-to-take-off) (understandably, but incorrectly, often misattributed to EA) that they need to "get comfortable" not owning their games. Then, in an act of short-sighted self-sabotage, Ubisoft proved the point by switching off *The Crew*, a game people had *paid for*, and that catalysed the [Stop Killing Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stop_Killing_Games) movement, which has since gathered over 1.4 million validated signatures in the EU and spun up [formal NGOs on two continents](https://www.notebookcheck.net/Stop-Killing-Games-launches-EU-and-US-watchdog-NGOs-as-Ross-Scott-says-I-think-we-re-going-to-win-this.1235433.0.html).

People are also sick of 100GB downloads for games that don't even work. They're sick of the abandonment of optimisation; not because developers can't optimise, but because the corporate engine behind them doesn't value the hours it takes. Retro gaming is having a renaissance, partly because the games actually load instantly and demonstrate real creativity, and partly because ten-year-old games still hold up, as the advances since are locked behind hardware nobody can afford.

There's a similar reaction among AI enthusiasts. LLMs are marvels, but they are nowhere near optimised. Chinese labs are already shipping models that run well on modest, even consumer, hardware. There's [open work on more efficient GPU designs](https://www.forbes.com/sites/davealtavilla/2026/04/28/tenstorrent-unveils-galaxy-ai-platform-targeting-scale-and-efficiency/), paired with software that does more with less. Gamers aren't the only ones demanding efficiency.

And it's part of a broader social movement: right-to-repair, the rise of self-hosting, the cyberdeck subculture, and the don't-kill-games campaign. The common thread is consumers who've simply had enough.

## The musical chairs of who gets to charge you

The hardware market tells a bigger story than just 'everything is too expensive'.

Intel is quietly shipping what are becoming the best value-for-money cards on the market. The [Arc Pro B70](https://videocardz.com/newz/intel-launches-arc-pro-b70-at-949-with-32gb-gddr6-memory) launched in March 2026 with 32GB of GDDR6 and an MSRP of $949 USD, under $1,500 AUD, explicitly aimed at local LLM inference, pitched on tokens-per-dollar against NVIDIA's workstation cards. You'd be hard pressed to find one at MSRP, but you _can_ get them for less than $2,000 AUD, which is still less than half the NVIDIA equivalent in terms of VRAM. They have the same ecosystem problem AMD once had, but two things are different: they're actually addressing it, and market forces are on their side. Intel has reportedly [killed discrete *gaming* GPUs for its next architecture entirely](https://www.tomshardware.com/pc-components/gpus/intels-arc-pro-b70-workstation-gpu-with-32gb-of-vram-gets-tested-in-games-roughly-twice-as-fast-as-arc-b580-on-average-beats-rtx-5060-ti-in-some-titles). They, too, have read the room.

With NVIDIA and Intel both walking away from the consumer gaming GPU, that leaves AMD. And the tragedy is that three or four years ago many gamers would have loved to jump from team green to team red, but AMD didn't invest in the ecosystem and their products never reached their full potential. AMD could have eaten NVIDIA's lunch, but instead they squandered the goodwill that they had. They still have more of that than NVIDIA (an admittedly low bar), but less *confidence*, which is not the same thing.

But GPUs aren't the whole story. Intel's CPUs aren't the exclusive kings they once were. AMD has outlasted every Intel challenger (remember Cyrix?) and has genuinely competed on performance for a long time, but the old reliability reputation lingers unfairly. There's pressure from ARM: Apple's M-series started it, now Snapdragon PCs continue it. Intel's lead is gone. **And yet Intel's CPU prices have not come down.** Will the RTX Spark also be expensive? Yes. Less than an RTX GPU, but still expensive.

That's the game, and the house always wins.

## The sovereignty angle (and why this matters more here)

There's a dimension to local-first AI that the pure consumerism framing misses. Personal AI on personal silicon isn't only driven by cost; it's the only path that doesn't route your thinking through someone else's ad-supported, jurisdiction-bound, politically-shapeable inference layer.

The CLOUD Act reaches a model running in OpenAI's data centre. It does not reach a model running on the laptop in front of you (for now). For anyone outside the United States, and especially for those of us watching [US tech policy lurch toward economic nationalism](https://www.beerdriven.dev/episodes/70/), the AI you actually control stops being a hobbyist's preference and starts being the only viable option. The RTX Spark, the Arc Pro B70, the whole local-inference turn - frame it as sovereignty and it reads very differently to an Australian audience than it does to Silicon Valley.

## Prices won't fall. We'll demand longevity instead.

I don't think we're ever going back to pre-Bitcoin consumer hardware pricing. But I do think we're going back to longer tails on investment. And maybe that's fine. We should remember that computers used to be obscenely expensive; it's the cheap years that were the anomaly. This is just a renormalisation.

But renormalisation comes with a demand attached. The reflexive consumer churn that defined tech — new phone every year, new GPU every cycle — is dying. People will still spend the money; they just won't spend it *every year*. They'll expect a computer to last five or ten years, and, crucially, they'll expect developers to keep catering to it. That's what all those movements I mentioned are really about: when an unstoppable force meets an immovable object, you get a compromise. We'll buy the absurdly expensive hardware, but we'll demand the right to repair it, and the right to make it last. I also suspect gamers will find a way of wringing gaming performance out of inference cards, just like LLM tinkerers do with gaming cards.

## Who actually loses

On the tech-titan side, the only long-term loser, I think, is OpenAI. Not dead (yet), but cornered, deserted by its two great patrons, holding compute commitments it can't pay against a consumer market discovering it doesn't want to rent what it can eventually own.

But the real losers are the ones who always lose: the consumers without access to paid tiers or their own silicon. The local-AI future is genuinely liberating, but only if you can afford the hardware to hold it. For everyone else, it's the same deal that search and social already taught them.

And we know how much appetite there is to do anything about it: Facebook knew its product was harming people, the internal documents proved it, the hearings happened, and then it was business as usual. _[The Social Reckoning](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt37510326/)_, in cinemas later this year and teased just last week, dramatises exactly that episode. The conversation around it is already, fittingly, about the reckoning that never came. 

That's the precedent.

The people who can afford their own silicon will opt out. Everyone else, which is most of humanity, gets the other version: their thinking routed through a layer that answers to advertisers and interests rather than to them.


## This is what comes next

For the first time, the consumer revolt and the enterprise reckoning are arriving *simultaneously*, not in sequence. Smartphones took the better part of a decade to go from launch to the quiet enterprise admission that the iPad-as-productivity-tool pitch never landed. AI is doing the whole arc, from subsidy and ubiquity, to enterprise disappointment and consumer reclamation, in about two years.

Everything that took Google twenty years and smartphones ten is happening to AI at once, in front of us, in real time.

Here is the factual argument, the one you can check. The bet for the last five years was enterprise transformation, and 'transformation' was always a polite word for headcount reduction. That bet has not paid out, and we can now say so without hedging. The research has been telling us for the better part of a year; the MIT numbers are only the most-quoted version of it. But research is easy to argue with, capital expenditure is not. When NVIDIA and Microsoft stop selling the AI-transformed workforce and start building silicon for your desk, that is the industry conceding the bet. And it isn't a pivot, however much the timing makes it look like one. Consumer was always the target. [Ballmer says as much](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/the-steve-ballmer-interview), that Microsoft began as a consumer company, and that one of his biggest regrets is not sustaining that. They aren't repositioning, they're arriving where they were always headed. The write-down accelerated the schedule, but it didn't change the destination.

So the real question isn't when, or if, the bubble will burst. It's if the enterprise windfall isn't coming, but the technology is real, ubiquitous, and expensive, who pays, and how? And we have the answer, twice over.

When search shifted the cost of being found onto us, we got SEO, then pay-to-rank, and now a first page you scroll straight past. When social media made our attention the product, we got engagement optimisation, and a machine that learned outrage travels furthest. Both times the value didn't vanish; it moved, from the thing offered _to_ us to the thing extracted _from_ us.

That is the historical imperative, and it is why this isn't speculation. Enshittification is not a risk AI might slide into. It is the completed pattern of every consumer technology that became too expensive to give away and too useful to abandon. The bubble doesn't burst, it just fills with shit. This is what comes next, because it is what has always come next.
