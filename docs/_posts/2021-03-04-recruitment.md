Before we talk about recruitment, let's just clear up one thing. Nobody wants to work for you just for the privilege of working for you. Like every rule there are, of course, exceptions. There are some organisations that are so prominent that some people desperately want to work for them (we'll talk about this a little more later). And there are some people who are so motivated by a particular organisation's mission or values that they feel strongly motivated to be a part of that.

But the likelihood that this is your scenario is infinitesimal. So, just to be clear and to double down on the point, let's say it again: Nobody want's to work for you just for the privilege of working for you.

First and foremost, people get up and go to work every day for the money. It is obviously true that there are other factors that motivate people - some people relish a particular challenge, or derive job satisfaction from the work they do. Others like the social aspect or camaraderie of their team. There are other things too. But these are all secondary. If people didn't need the money, they wouldn't come and work for you.

That may seem cynical, and there are certainly exceptions. But it's important to frame the rest of the discussion in these terms. With that in mind, let's look at some phrases from actual job ads. These are all for tech roles, and it may be that this style is particular to tech, but the sentiment is relevant to any field.

> Looking for a gun DevOps engineer

And:

> Looking for a rockstar full stack developer

These two phrases essentially make the same point - we want someone *really* good. Ok. But a rockstar? If you hire a "rockstar" full stack developer, are you going to treat them like a rockstar? Will you give them a named parking space right near the door, or roll out the red carpet for them?

I'm particularly fond of this one:

> We value people who are always looking to push themselves to the next level.

That seems innocuous, and in fairness is better than working for a company that prefers people to stagnate (believe it or not they exist - I know from personal experience). But this particular ad said nothing about how they as an employer would *support* the growth of their employees Or how they would ensure that they themselves always offered that next level for their staff to push themselves to.

The reason I like the phrasing of that last one is because it offers a neat summary of the problem with all of these. And that is that they are all about what the employer values, what the employer wants, with nothing about what they are offering in return.

The simple answer is remuneration. Employers often want "the best of the best", but fail to recognise that this means actually *being* the best of the best employers too. If you want to attract the best staff, you have to be prepared to offer the best salaries too.

Don't get me wrong, there are plenty of other motivators. I know many people, myself included, who have taken roles below their earning potential because they value other benefits. If you can't offer the best salary, what *can* you offer?
